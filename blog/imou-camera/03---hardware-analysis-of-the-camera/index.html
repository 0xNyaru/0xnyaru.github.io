[Imou Camera] Hardware Analysis of the camera

2.1 Introduction#

After extracting the firmware update from the Imou Ranger 2C camera and analyzing the behavior of the Imou Life mobile application, the next step is to examine the hardware side of the device.

This section focuses on a full hardware analysis: disassembling the camera, identifying electronic components, mapping physical interfaces (UART, JTAG, etc.), and evaluating the potential for low-level system access. The goal is to document the internal architecture, identify potential weaknesses or exploitable entry points, and better understand the hardware design of the device from a security research perspective.

2.4 Component analysis#

2.4.1 ATBM6062#

According to its datasheet ↗, the ATBM6062 is indeed a 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi and Bluetooth LE 5.0 module.
Unfortunately, documentation for this component is very limited, and there is little more to extract from publicly available sources.

2.4.2 SSC332#

The SSC332 is a multimedia SoC from Sigmastar (formerly Sigma Designs, later spun off from MStar). It is commonly used in low-cost IP cameras.

While no public datasheet for this specific model appears to exist, a closely related model—the SSC333 ↗ documented.

Its characteristics include:

  • ARM Cortex-A7 single-core CPU
  • Support for various encoding/processing pipelines
  • Multiple interfaces: UART, SPI, I²C, GPIO
  • Several boot options:
    • SPI NOR / NAND
    • SD card
  • Embedded SPI NOR/NAND flash
  • 512 MB DDR2 memory

2.5 Debug port identification#

Since the processor is ARM-based, the debug pins could correspond to UART or SWD. While 4-pin SWD headers are uncommon, they remain a possibility.

To verify this, it’s time to pull out the multimeter and search for GND. As explained in the Silvercrest gateway teardown, using a multimeter makes it easy to locate ground pins.

Why only look for GND? Because once ground is identified, a logic analyzer can be safely connected to detect the protocol automatically, without having to redo all the voltage and resistance calculations demonstrated in the gateway analysis.

Using the multimeter in continuity mode, GND can be found by placing the black probe on one of the PCB screws, then probing each pin with the red probe. Pin 3 (counting from the square pad) emits a beep, confirming ground.
